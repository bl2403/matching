General Structure of the Experiment

As you can see, there are 5 subjects who will be participating in the experiment. At the very beginning of this lab session, all subjects are assigned into types which we call Type 1, Type 2, Type 3, Type 4, and Type 5. After you finish your decision-making, you will be replaced by five other subjects in the next experimental session, and these five subjects will also be assigned into the same five types. Now in this experiment, you will participate in four sets of decision problems, and we will explain the details of these decision problems in order.

i The Matching Scheme

In the matching scheme each subject is required to submit a ranking over the three objects. A ranking is simply a list of objects that a subject states that she chooses first, second, or third, and the ranking must contain all three objects. In other words, she cannot state object A is both her first and second choice. Notice that this ranking can be different from your actual preference. For instance, in the figure above, the subject prefers object C over A over B, but she is free to rank object A as her first choice and C as her second choice.

We will now describe how the matching scheme works. As you will see, the object you will be matched with depends on the rankings you submit, the number of subjects wanting the various objects, the priorities of the subjects wanting the same object, and the lottery used to break ties when more subjects with the same priority want the same subject.

The matching mechanism has the following steps:

(Step 1) The computer will randomly generate a ranking of all subjects which will later serve as the only tie-breaking lottery, if necessary.

(Step 2) The computer gathers all the rankings submitted by the subjects and then takes each subject's first ranked choice and make an application for him or her to that object.

(Step 3) For each object, if the number of applicants is less than or equal to the available units of the object, all applicants will be temporarily matched to that object. Otherwise, the object will be temporarily matched to subjects by using their priority rankings from the highest to lowest until all units are taken. If two applicants have the same priority ranking, then the tie-breaking lottery will be used and the subjects with higher tie-breaking number will be admitted first. Low priority applicants or low tie-breaking applicants in excess of the number of available units will be left unmatched temporarily.

(Step 4) If at the end of the first round all subjects are matched to objects, the temporary assignment outcome will be finalized and the mechanism terminates. If there are any unmatched subjects, a second round starts where all unmatched subjects are sent to their second ranked objects.

(Step 5) For each object that receives new applicants, the computer compares the number of its remaining units with that of new applicants. If the remaining units are more than the number of new applicants, all new applicants are temporarily matched to that object. Otherwise, these new applicants will be pooled together with the subjects who are currently matched to that object from the previous round. Among these new applicants and matched applicants, the object will be temporarily assigned to applicants again by using their priority ranking and tie-breaking ranking from the highest to the lowest until all available units in the object are exhausted. Note, it is possible that a subject was temporarily matched to the object in the previous round but is left unmatched in this round, because his priority or tie-breaking lottery is not high enough and he gets replaced by a new applicant with higher priority or tie-breaking number.

(Step 6) At the end of the second round, if all subjects are matched with a object, then the temporary matching outcome will be finalized and the mechanism is terminated. However, if there are still unmatched subjects, then a third round will start where all unmatched subjects are sent to their next ranked object.

(Step 7) This process is repeated until all subjects are matched with an object. Then the temporary matching outcome finalizes and the matching mechanism terminates, and all subjects receive the object they were matched with.

The detail of this matching scheme will still be available to you throughout relevant part of the experiment, at the bottom of the screen.

Instruction for Decision Problem 3

In this part of the experiment, you will be asked to provide advice to some subjects in the group that participates after you. You will be given the advice receiver's preference over the objects and asked to give suggested ranking and verbal reasoning to support your advice. Please keep in mind that these preference types are hypothetical; in other words, they may or may not actually exist in the experiment. But you can be sure that at least one of these preference types actually exists. You will be given preferences different from that of yours, so please note that when you give advice to a particular type of subject, you must put yourself in their position to give advice. Do not simply repeat what you did in the experiment. Consider what you would have done if you were of that particular preference type. A typical advice giving page looks like the following figure.

As you can see, at the top of the page, your preference and priority will be repeated. Then your advisee's preferences over the objects and priorities will be provided. And your task is to choose what ranking you suggested him or her to take and provide verbal reasoning for it. Your reasoning should be concise and pertinent.

Finally, your payoff from advice giving is 1/2 of the payoff of the subject who receives your advice. If multiple subjects receive your advice, your payoff will be determined by randomly selecting one from such subjects. Therefore, you should provide advice to all your advisees to the best of your ability.

Instruction for Decision Problem 4

This is the last part of the experiment and is totally different from the previous problems. There are two tasks you need to complete. The first task is for you to choose the more preferred options from the ten pairs of lotteries. A typical lottery comparison page looks like the following figure.

A lottery is in the following form: you may have probability p of receiving m ECU and probability 1-p of receiving n ECU. You will make ten choices and for each choice you only need to indicate which lottery you prefer more. Once you have made all ten decisions, click the "Next" button. Your final payoff is also dependent on your decisions here: Each lottery pair has equal probability of being selected and the computer will run your more preferred lottery in that pair to determine your payoff from this decision problem.

The second task contains three quiz questions commonly used in economics experiments on decision making. You should answer according to the best of your ability.
